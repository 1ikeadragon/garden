There is a particular kind of dishonesty in only sharing finished work. The blog post about the system that works. The architecture diagram with clean arrows. The retrospective that reads like a story with a beginning, a middle, and an end, as though the author knew the ending from the start.

Nobody knows the ending from the start. The actual process is: try a thing, it does not work, try another thing, it mostly works, discover an edge case at 2am, fix the edge case in a way that creates two new edge cases, fix those, ship it, discover a third edge case in production, fix that, and then write a blog post that makes it sound like you had a plan.

Building in public is the practice of showing the messy part. Not the victory lap. The part where you are confused. The part where the approach you were sure about turns out to be wrong. The part where you read the error message six times before noticing the typo.

This is uncomfortable. It is also more useful than any finished-work post, because the person reading it is almost certainly in the messy part right now. They do not need to see your clean architecture. They need to see that the clean architecture was, three weeks ago, a sketch on the back of a receipt that you were not sure about.

A digital garden like this one is an exercise in building in public. The [[notes]] section is full of half-formed ideas, notes that link to other notes that link back, thoughts that contradict each other. That is the point. The garden is not a portfolio. It is a workshop, and workshops are messy.

The connection to [[the-cost-of-shortcuts]]: the temptation to only show finished work is the same temptation as the shortcut. It looks cleaner. It feels more professional. And it hides exactly the information that would have been most useful to the next person.

See also [[on reading slowly]] — the same patience required to read slowly is required to build publicly. Both require tolerating incompleteness.
