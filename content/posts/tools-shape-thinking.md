You do not think the same way in every tool. The text editor you use, the programming language you write in, the note-taking system you keep — each one imposes a shape on your thoughts, and you do not notice the shape until you switch tools and feel something shift.

Write the same idea in a linear document and in a hyperlinked garden. In the document it becomes an argument: premise, development, conclusion. In the garden it becomes a node: connected to other nodes, reachable from multiple directions, with no prescribed reading order. The idea is the same. The shape it takes is different. The shape changes what you notice about it.

This is McLuhan's "the medium is the message" applied to the smallest unit of intellectual work. The IDE with inline type errors makes you think about types differently than the editor without them. The language with pattern matching makes you think about data differently than the language with if-else chains. The terminal with a fast feedback loop makes you think about experiments differently than the one where each build takes four minutes.

The practical consequence: ==choose tools deliberately==, because you are also choosing the shape of your thinking. If you want to think in connections, use a tool that makes connections cheap. If you want to think in sequences, use a tool that makes sequences natural. If you want to think in types, use a language where types are first-class.

The trap is choosing tools for aesthetics or social proof. A tool that looks good in a screenshot but does not match the shape of your thinking is worse than an ugly tool that does. The ugliest tool I have ever used — a tiling terminal multiplexer from 2003 — shaped my thinking more than any polished IDE, because its constraints forced me to decompose problems into pieces that each fit in a single pane.

[[the-real-bubble-isnt-ai|The real bubble]] is partly a tool-shape problem: when the tool is an LLM that generates text fluently, the shape of your thinking becomes "generate text fluently," which is not the same as "think clearly." The fluency is the tool's. The thinking should be yours.

Related: [[chestertons fence]] — the principle applies to tools too. Before switching away from a tool, understand what shape it gave your thinking and whether the replacement preserves it.
