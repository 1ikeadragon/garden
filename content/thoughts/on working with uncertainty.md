Most of the interesting work happens before you know if you are on the right track. The uncertainty is not a bug in the process. It is the process.

The temptation is to wait — wait for more data, wait for a clearer signal, wait for someone to tell you the answer. Waiting feels responsible. It feels like due diligence. In practice it is often a way of avoiding the discomfort of acting on incomplete information.

The alternative is not recklessness. It is provisional commitment: choose a direction, move, pay attention to what happens, adjust. The cost of being wrong-and-correcting is almost always lower than the cost of waiting-until-certain, because certainty takes longer to arrive than most people think, and the world does not pause while you deliberate.

This is related to [[on saying yet.|yet]] — the willingness to act despite not being ready is the same muscle as the willingness to say "I don't know this yet" and start learning. Both require tolerating the gap between where you are and where you need to be, and both require moving into the gap rather than standing at the edge of it.

It is also why [[on-building-in-public|building in public]] works: by exposing the uncertain, in-progress version of your work, you get feedback earlier, which reduces uncertainty faster than waiting would.

The engineers I respect most are not the ones who are always right. They are the ones who are comfortable being wrong, loudly, and then fixing it quickly. The speed of the correction matters more than the accuracy of the first attempt.
