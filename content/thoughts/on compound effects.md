The thing about compound effects is that they are boring in both directions. A 1% improvement per day is boring. A 1% degradation per day is boring. After a year, one has tripled and the other has been reduced to almost nothing, and at no point during the year did either feel dramatic.

This is why [[silent failures]] are so dangerous in engineering and in life. A system that degrades by 0.1% per week looks fine on any given Monday. After a year it has lost half its performance, and the loss happened so slowly that the new baseline feels normal.

The same principle in reverse is why [[on reading slowly|reading slowly]] works. One book read carefully is not dramatic. Ten books read carefully over a year, each one building on the last, each one connected to the others — that compounds. Ten books skimmed in a month does not compound. It dissipates.

The hardest part is the patience. Compound effects require you to keep doing the boring thing long past the point where anyone is impressed, including yourself. The second hardest part is noticing the negative compounds — the small bad habits, the minor technical debt, the slight degradations — before they have accumulated past the point of easy repair.

> Every day you do not address a small problem, you are choosing to address a large problem later.
