There is a trap people my age keep falling into, where we confuse *having read* a thing with *having learned* from it. The Kindle counter ticks up. The highlights accumulate in some service nobody opens. The to-read list never shrinks, because each book finished produces three more books recommended, and the ambient anxiety of being behind on the discourse rewards opening a new tab over finishing the paragraph in front of you.

I do not think most of what I have *read* in the last year left a mark. I think a very small handful of things did. The rest passed through me the way water passes through a sieve.

The trick, if there is one, is to notice when a page is resisting you and to stay there. Read it again. Read it aloud. Copy a sentence into a [[lines|file of lines]] and come back tomorrow. The compound interest of understanding only accrues if you let a thing sit long enough for it to bother you.

>  Slowness is a form of respect for the text, and also for yourself — it says the thing you are reading is worth a second pass, and your attention is worth more than the ambient rush.

A corollary, mostly to myself: the book I finish in three hours and forget in two days did not actually cost me three hours. It cost me the three hours, plus the ones I will spend re-reading it next year because some vague shape of its argument is rattling around in my head and I cannot place the source.

Read slower. Finish fewer things. Say, out loud, the thing the author was trying to tell you. If you can't, you did not read it yet.
